Exploring the Inequities in the United States Asylum System

They told me, ‘you don’t have any rights here, and you don’t have any rights to stay with your son.’ …For me I died at that moment. They ripped my heart out of me. …For me, it would have been better if I had dropped dead. For me, the world ended at that point. …How can a mother not have the right to be with her son?”

Asylum Seekers in the United States

Asylum laws are an increasingly relevant political topic: globally and nationally. With recent violence and political turmoil in places like Syria and Venezuela, global asylum seekers, those fleeing persecution in their home countries, have been steadily increasing. The UN estimates that today there are 3.1 million asylum seekers worldwide. In the United States, asylum applications have fluctuated in the past 4 years.

Most notably, there was a sharp decline in total and defensive asylum applications from January 2017 to April 2017. This is the same time of Trump's controversial 'Muslim Ban' and Executive Order 13767, which made it more difficult to claim asylum at the border.



During his campaign and tenure in office, Trump has repeatedly called out asylum seekers for 'scamming' the system. This highlights one of the injustices that asylum seekers face when seeking refuge in the United States.

Vulnerability to Political Climate

The Executive Branch's rhetoric around asylum seekers can influence policy and popular discourse. For people seeking asylum, their legal process and outcomes may change drastically under different presidents.

For affirmative asylum seekers, it may be getting more difficult to navigate the complex legal system. This is shown through the increased number of applicants who are referred to immigration court because they filed too late and those who are being denied asylum because they were not present at their interview.

Interestingly, while ~12% more applicants are classified as "No Show Denial" from 2015 to 2019, there is only a ~3% increase in affirmative applicants who did not show to their interview for the same time period, as seen in the graph below.

Besides the vulnerability to popular discourse as established by the media and key political figures, asylum seekers are often left on their own to navigate the complicated immigration bureaucracy. This is the root to another injustice faced by those seeking asylum in the US.

Bureaucratic Inefficiencies

Even for well versed legal experts, the asylum system is complicated. For those entering the country without any information and potentially no grasp on the language, it is no doubt extremely confusing.

Use the animation below to explore the known and unknown legal paths of asylum seekers in 2018. Each dot represents 100 cases. Click on the stops to see where asylum seekers go from there.

The cases stuck on the edge represent asylum seekers for whom the outcome of their case is not known.
The data available on asylum seekers leaves many gaps where numbers do not quite add up. It is impossible to know how many of the unaccounted for cases are in detention.

As shown above, most asylum cases are referred to an immigration judge who will make a final ruling on their case. This can often take years, as there are hundreds and thousands of backlogged cases.

Additionally, different immigration courts and officials across the country often have very distinct outcomes. This is another way in which asylum seekers are not afforded the American ideal of due process.


The most vulnerable populations are subject to an unjust and voltile system, both depriving them of their rights to a fair trial and due process of the law as is stated in the United States Constitution.

Asylum Seeker

According to the Refugee Act of 1980: anyone who “is unable or unwilling to return to… that country because of persecution or a well-founded fear of persecution on account of race, religion, nationality, membership in a particular social group, or political opinion”

Defensive Asylum

Someone who is undocumented in the country, apprehended by immigration officials (ICE), and at risk of deportation or someone who is apprehended at the border and asks to be granted asylum.

Credible Fear Review

Defensive asylum seekers must prove that they have a credible fear of persecution were they to return home. This is an intital screening interview done with an immigration officer in the event that the applicant is in the 'Expedited Removals' process. If they are found to have a credible fear, then they are referred to an immigration judge who will make the final decision on their case.

Affirmative Asylum

Someone who is in the US on another visa and wants to change their status to be an asylee. They must file an application within one year of their other visa expiring.

Affirmative Asylum Interview

Affirmative asylum applicants are interviewed by immigration officers to determine if they eligible for asylum status. If the immigration officer does not think they are eligible, they will be referred to an immigration judge who will make the final decision on their case.
